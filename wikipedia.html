Elizabeth Woolridge Grant (born June 21, 1985), known professionally as Lana Del Rey, is an American singer-songwriter. Her music is noted for its cinematic quality and exploration of tragic romance, glamour, and melancholia, with frequent references to contemporary pop culture and 1950s–70s Americana.[1] Her vintage Hollywood glamour aesthetic is presented not only in her music, but also in her music videos. She is the recipient of various accolades, including two Brit Awards, two Billboard Women in Music, two MTV Europe Music Awards, and a Satellite Award, in addition to nominations for six Grammy Awards and a Golden Globe Award. Variety honored her at their Hitmakers Awards for being "one of the most influential singer-songwriters of the 21st century". Rolling Stone placed Del Rey on their list of the 200 Greatest Singers of All Time (2023), and Rolling Stone UK named her The Greatest American Songwriter of the 21st century (2023).[2][3] Time magazine named Del Rey one of the 100 most influential people in the world in 2012

Early life

Elizabeth Woolridge Grant was born on June 21, 1985,[11] in Manhattan, New York City,[12] to Robert England Grant Jr., a copywriter at Grey Group, and Patricia Ann "Pat" (née Hill), an account executive at the same organization.[13][14][15] She has a younger sister, Caroline "Chuck" Grant,[16] and a younger brother, Charlie Grant.[17][18] She was raised Roman Catholic and is of Scottish descent.[19][20] When she was one year old, the family moved to Lake Placid, New York.[21] In Lake Placid, her father worked for a furniture company before becoming an entrepreneurial domain investor;[22] her mother worked as a schoolteacher.[23] There, she attended St. Agnes School in her elementary years[18] and began singing in her church choir, where she was the cantor.[18][24] She attended the high school where her mother taught for one year,[23] but when she was 14[25] or 15,[26] her parents sent her to Kent School[27] to get sober from alcoholism. Grant shared in an interview: "That's really why I got sent to boarding school aged 14—to get sober."[25] Her uncle, an admissions officer at the school, secured her financial aid to attend.[28] According to Grant, she had trouble making friends during much of her teenage and early adult years.[29][30] She has said she was preoccupied with death from a young age, and its role in her feelings of anxiety and alienation:

Career

In fall 2004, at age 19, Grant enrolled at Fordham University in The Bronx where she majored in philosophy, with an emphasis on metaphysics.[12] She has said she chose to study the subject because it "bridged the gap between God and science... I was interested in God and how technology could bring us closer to finding out where we came from and why."[12] In spring 2005, while still in college, Del Rey registered a seven-track extended play with the United States Copyright Office; the application title was Rock Me Stable with another title, Young Like Me, also listed.[33] A second extended play, From the End, was also recorded under Del Rey's stage name at the time, May Jailer.[34] Between 2005 and 2006, she recorded an acoustic album, Sirens, under the May Jailer project,[34] which leaked on the internet in mid-2012.[3

Del Rey graduated from Fordham with a Bachelor of Arts degree in philosophy in 2008,[27] after which she released a three-track EP, Kill Kill, as Lizzy Grant, featuring production by Kahne.[38] She explained: "David asked to work with me only a day after he got my demo. He is known as a producer with a lot of integrity and who had an interest in making music that wasn't just pop."[39] Meanwhile, Del Rey was doing community outreach work for homeless individuals and drug addicts;[12] she had become interested in community service work in college, when she "took a road trip across the country to paint and rebuild houses on a Native American reservation".[18][40] Of choosing a stage name for her feature debut album, she said: "I wanted a name I could shape the music towards. I was going to Miami quite a lot at the time, speaking a lot of Spanish with my friends from Cuba—Lana Del Rey reminded us of the glamour of the seaside. It sounded gorgeous coming off the tip of the tongue."[41] The name was also inspired by actress Lana Turner and the Ford Del Rey sedan, produced and sold in Brazil in the 1980s.[42] Initially she used the alternate spelling Lana Del Ray, the name under which her self-titled debut album was released in January 2010.[37] Her father helped with the marketing of the album,[43] which was available for purchase on iTunes for a brief period before being withdrawn in April 2010.[37] Kahne and Nichtern both said that Del Rey bought the rights back from 5 Points, as she wanted it out of circulation to "stifle future opportunities to distribute it—an echo of rumors the action was part of a calculated strategy"

Impact

Del Rey has been mentioned as an influence by a number of artists including Billie Eilish,[286] Lauren Jauregui,[287] Kevin Abstract,[288][289] Maggie Lindemann,[290] XXXTentacion[291] and Olivia Rodrigo.[292] Billboard credited Born to Die with being one of the main catalysts for pop music's shift from an overall brash EDM tone to a moodier, hip-hop-inflected palette in the mid-2010s, and opined that Del Rey is indispensable to the decade's pop music, having influenced alternative-leaning pop artists such as Lorde, Halsey, Banks, Sky Ferreira, Father John Misty, Sia, Miley Cyrus, Selena Gomez and Taylor Swift.[293] Time magazine named Del Rey one of the 100 most influential people in the world in 2012.[294] In 2019, Billboard included "Born to Die" amongst the 100 songs that defined the 2010s, adding that it marked "a sonic shift that completely changed the pop landscape".[295] Norman Fucking Rockwell! was named one of the 500 Greatest Albums of All Time by Rolling Stone.[7] The Washington Post listed Del Rey as the only musician on their "Decade of Influence" list.[296] Pitchfork named her one of the greatest living songwriters of the US.[297] The Guardian declared Del Rey's own "pure female haze" a "hallmark of the defiant female pop stars to come".[298] Her YouTube and Vevo pages have combined views of over four billion.[299][300][301] In 2022, New York University's Clive Davis Institute of Recorded Music launched the fall semester course "Topics in Recorded Music: Lana Del Rey", which deals with Del Rey's music.[302] Rolling Stone ranked Del Rey at number 175 on its 2023 list of the 200 Greatest Singers of All Time.[3] Rolling Stone UK named her The Greatest American Songwriter of the 21st century (2023)
